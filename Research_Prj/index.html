Research Foundation

The Archive
Under Restriction

Every archive operates inside restrictions it didn't choose, restrictions written into corporate infrastructure, legal access rules, and platform ownership. This project sets aside whether an archive can survive and asks instead how its context keeps evolving once it's already built inside someone else's rules.

01 · Research Questions

"How can computational interfaces let an archive's context keep evolving past the restrictions it was built inside?"

Every archive, physical or digital, operates under some restriction it didn't design, whether a legal order, a corporate terms of service, or a platform's ownership. This research doesn't ask whether that restriction can be removed. It asks what computational rules, not just what content, could keep an archive's context alive and legible in spite of it, and who gets to set those rules.

And a harder question follows. Does digital "interactivity" actually loosen that restriction, or does it just relocate the gatekeeping into an interface, where you can only contribute in the shapes a form, a tag menu, or a comment box was built to accept?

Context isn't static, so it treats time as a design variable, not just visibility. What if a contribution's exposure isn't fixed the moment it's written, but governed by a rule instead, one where something sealed resurfaces once enough related entries accumulate around it, something unvisited fades from view without ever being deleted, and something anonymous can be reconnected to its author only under conditions set in advance? That's a question for an algorithm, not a form.

04 · Historical Lineage

A ledger of the restrictions archives operate under, and a second lineage of how they evolve past them.

First, the record of restriction, not fire or decay, but the legal orders and corporate infrastructure that decide who can reach an archive and how its context can be used. This isn't the main argument here, it's the condition everything else is designed against.

05 · Situated Technology

Every interface decides what counts as a contribution.

A tag field, a comment box, a reaction bar with five icons, each one quietly defines the shapes a contribution is allowed to take. The Uncensored Library shows the same logic at the level of the platform itself. It's hosted inside Minecraft, made by Mojang, owned by Microsoft, a company with its own history of complying with government data requests. Whether people are contributing content or just visiting it, they're doing so inside someone else's designed and governed space.

This work treats interface design itself as archival policy. Every dropdown, form field, and moderation rule is a decision about who gets to speak in the record, made before any user ever arrives.

That policy doesn't stop at the interface. An algorithm that decides which memory resurfaces, which stays sealed, or which connects to which is making the same kind of decision as a locked door, just running continuously instead of once. Treating that as a design object, not an implementation detail, is what makes this a computational question rather than an archival one.

The one interface decision kept here is a visibility choice, open, sealed, or anonymous, offered to each contributor at the moment of writing. Everything else follows Fuller's rule, nothing is excluded from the record itself, sealed or not, seen or not.

06 · Methods

How this gets built

01
Investigating a written contribution format, a memory plus one visibility choice, open, sealed, or anonymous.
02
Prototyping a continuous chronological log, timestamped, unedited, nothing excluded, following Fuller's Chronofile rule.
03
Testing a translation from digital to physical, how an open entry prints legibly and a sealed entry prints as a fold, redaction, or blackout mark.
04
Drawing on the restriction dataset as context, evidence for why designing around who gets excluded matters more than designing for indestructibility.
05
Investigating computational rules for how entries relate over time, decay for the unvisited, resurfacing once related entries accumulate, connection by theme rather than by date alone.

08 · Rhetorical Argument

An archive proves it's alive by how its context keeps evolving past the restrictions it was built inside, not by how well it resists loss, and it stays honest by refusing to judge what's worth keeping.

09 · Toward a Capstone

A gesture, not a plan yet

Research direction

This research may culminate in an interactive archival system that investigates computational models of participation, authorship, and visibility, where a contribution's exposure isn't fixed at the moment it's written, but governed by a rule that can let it decay, resurface, or connect to other entries as the archive grows. The open question is what that rule should be, and who gets to set it, not yet which interface it runs through.

One possible implementation

The Two Way Archive. Visitors write a personal memory and choose how it's seen, open, sealed, or anonymous, logged permanently in chronological order, following Fuller's Chronofile rule that nothing gets excluded from the record, seen or not. This is a single fixed rule, one restriction the contributor sets instead of a platform. A stronger version would treat visibility as something that can change over time rather than only at submission, which is the direction methods 02 and 05 are meant to test before this gets treated as a final form.

10 · The Challenge

Technical

I don't yet have strong skills in building even a simple digital logging system or in driving a plotter or printer from live input. This will mean learning basic logic for connecting a web form to a database, and physical output such as plotting or printing, over the semester.

Design

Designing a sealed or redacted mark that reads as genuinely withheld rather than merely decorative, and keeping the growing scroll legible as a single object rather than visual noise as entries accumulate.

11 · Exhibition Components

Two proposals

Sketches for testing the research direction in section 09, not commitments to a final form. The fixed three part visibility choice below is the simplest version to test with; a version governed by a rule, where visibility can change after submission, is the one worth building toward.

Printed Vertical Drawing · "The Chronofile, Left Open"

A tall scroll printed top to bottom in strict chronological order, exactly like Fuller's own Chronofile. Open memories are set in legible type, sealed ones rendered as folds, redactions, or blackout bars, anonymous ones marked but unattributed. The top of the scroll can carry a condensed version of the restriction ledger as fixed context; the lower portion grows live during the review as visitors contribute, so the piece is visibly still being written when the jury sees it.

  • Format, a single long vertical print or plot, roughly mural scale, suspended from the Avery 100 rails, printed or plotted in sections as contributions accumulate.
  • Content, entries from the written memory contribution system, in the order they arrive.
  • Visual rule, legible type for open entries, folded or redacted marks for sealed ones. The reader can see how much of the archive they're not allowed to read.

Material and Spatial Gesture · "Writing Station"

A small physical station, paper, or a simple keyboard or input device, where a visitor writes one personal memory and picks open, sealed, or anonymous before submitting. A visible readout (a counter, a printed timestamp, a light) confirms the memory has entered the log the moment it's submitted, whether or not it will ever be shown. The fixed choice is the version to test first; a computational rule that lets that choice change over time, rather than lock in at submission, is the direction this gesture is meant to lead toward.

  • Form, a simple writing surface or input device with a three part visibility choice and a confirmation readout.
  • Placement, alongside the drawing during final review, feeding it directly.
  • Function, makes the moment of contribution, and its provisional exposure, physically present in the room.
12 · Precedents & References

Who else is doing this, and everything cited along the way

Every project, reading, and precedent cited across this site, gathered in one place. Not exhaustive, a working bibliography for an interim stage.

Community of practice

Reporters Without Borders & BlockWorks · The Uncensored Library (2020)

The direct precedent for this work, and the one it argues against, an archive that is top down and curated, built on infrastructure it doesn't control. RSF adds content and visitors move through it; they don't shape it.

Rick Prelinger · Lost Landscapes

Live audiences collectively narrate archival film in real time, participation with almost no interface at all, entirely social and physical.

Cooper Hewitt Pen

Visitors physically tap objects during a museum visit to build a personal digital collection afterward, a genuine loop between physical and digital contribution.

Zooniverse (2007) & Smithsonian Transcription Center (2013)

Turn the public into fellow archivists, but only within the tags, fields, and forms the platform provides. A useful cautionary case as much as a model.

Community archives movement · Michelle Caswell

Archives built and held by the communities they document, a direct rejection of the single, external custodian. Caswell's writing ("Toward a Survivor-Centered Approach to Records Documenting Human Rights Abuse"; "Community Archives, Community Spaces") is the scholarly anchor for this movement.

Mukurtu CMS & Local Contexts · Kimberly Christen (2010)

An archive platform governed by the community, and a labeling system that let Indigenous communities set their own access protocols instead of accepting a platform's default open or closed setting. The clearest working precedent for the central move made in this work, restriction chosen by the people documented, not imposed by whoever hosts the archive.

Library Freedom Project

A reminder that "who gets to shape or even see the record" has been contested since long before digital archives existed.

Buckminster Fuller · the Dymaxion Chronofile, 1917 to 1983

A direct model for the logging logic used here, inclusion without curation.

Theory & critical frameworks

Jacques Derrida · Archive Fever (1995)

Argues no archive is neutral; the archive's structure already decides what can be remembered. Foundational for treating restriction as built into the archive, not incidental to it.

Michel Foucault · The Archaeology of Knowledge (1969)

Not about archives specifically, but background for how systems of knowledge police what counts as a valid statement in the first place.

Johanna Drucker · "Humanities Approaches to Interface"

Interface as an interpretive layer, not a neutral window, direct support for section 05's argument that every interface decision is a restriction decision.

Shannon Mattern · "A City Is Not a Computer"

Infrastructure as a hidden layer of governance, useful for thinking about the corporate/infrastructural column of the restriction ledger.

Andrew Flinn · writing on community archives

Access as something communities negotiate for themselves, not something a platform grants them.

Nicolas Bourriaud · Relational Aesthetics (1998)

Framing of art as social interaction rather than a fixed object.

Nina Simon · The Participatory Museum (2010)

Institutions designing for visitors to contribute, not just consume.
